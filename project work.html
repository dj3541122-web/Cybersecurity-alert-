‎

‎In 2026, cyber threats have become a daily reality for Nigerians. Nigerian organisations recorded an average of 4,701 cyber attacks per week in January 2026 — the highest in Africa. This represents a 12% year-on-year increase and a rise from 4,622 attacks per week in December 2025.



‎WHAT IS CYBERSPACE

‎

‎Cyberspace refers to the virtual digital environment created by interconnected computer networks, the internet, and digital devices where people communicate, conduct business, store data, and interact. It includes everything from social media platforms and banking apps to government portals and mobile money systems. In simple terms, it is the invisible world where our online lives exist — a space without physical borders but filled with real risks.

‎

‎As Nigeria’s digital economy booms with mobile money, fintech apps, and online government services, the attack surface in this cyberspace keeps growing rapidly, especially in bustling hubs like Lagos, Abuja, and Port Harcourt. From sophisticated AI-powered phishing scams that perfectly mimic your bank manager’s voice to ransomware attacks that lock entire systems, the risks are no longer abstract headlines — they directly affect businesses, families, and daily transactions.

This is not mere fear-mongering.It is a practical *cybersecurity* alert filled with Nigeria-specific context and actionable steps you can start implementing today to safeguard your data, finances, and peace of mind.

‎TYPES OF CYBER SCAMS COMMON IN NIGERIA IN 2026

‎

‎Cyber scams have become more sophisticated and widespread. Here are the major types targeting Nigerians:

‎

‎Cyber scams have become more sophisticated and widespread. Here are the major types targeting Nigerians:

‎


‎1. Phishing Scams

‎Attackers send fake emails, SMS, or WhatsApp messages pretending to be from trusted organisations like banks, NITDA, or INEC. The goal is to trick you into clicking malicious links or revealing sensitive information such as passwords, BVN, or NIN.

‎

‎2. Voice Cloning and Deepfake Scams

‎Criminals use AI to clone the voice of family members, bank officials, or bosses to demand urgent money transfers or sensitive data. Video deepfakes are also rising, making impersonation even more convincing.

‎

‎3. SIM-Swap Scams

‎Fraudsters hijack your phone number through your network provider and use it to bypass two-factor authentication on banking and email accounts.

‎

‎4. Business Email Compromise (BEC)

‎Hackers impersonate company executives or suppliers via email to trick employees or business owners into making large fraudulent payments.

‎

‎5. Romance Scams and Investment Frauds

‎Scammers build fake relationships online or promise huge returns on fake investment schemes (crypto, forex, or “business opportunities”) to defraud victims of large sums.

‎

‎6. Ransomware Attacks

‎Malware that locks your files or systems and demands payment (usually in cryptocurrency) to restore access. This is increasingly hitting small businesses and government agencies.

‎

‎7. Account Takeover Scams

‎Using stolen credentials from data breaches, fraudsters gain access to your bank accounts, fintech wallets, or social media profiles.

‎

‎These scams exploit trust, urgency, and fear — common tactics that continue to succeed because many people lack basic awareness.

‎

‎WHY CYBER SECURITY THREATS ARE EXPLODING IN NIGERIA IN 2026

‎

‎Nigeria’s rapid digital transformation, while bringing convenience through platforms like OPay, PalmPay, and Moniepoint, has unfortunately created more entry points for cybercriminals. Key drivers include:

‎

‎1. AI supercharging attacks: Criminals now use generative AI to craft hyper-personalized phishing emails, voice-cloned calls, and deepfake videos. These make scams incredibly convincing and much harder to spot.

‎

‎2. Surge in identity theft: Infostealer malware activity rose 66% in 2025, affecting nearly 29% of Nigerian users. AI-driven phishing and impersonation attacks are projected to intensify by up to 70% in 2026.

‎

‎3. Ransomware evolution: Attackers now employ “double” or “triple extortion” — encrypting systems, stealing data, and threatening to leak sensitive information.

‎

‎The economic toll is heavy. Nigeria has lost over $500 million annually to cybercrime in recent years, with broader estimates for 2025 reaching $1.2–1.5 billion when including indirect costs like downtime and reputational damage. Financial institutions alone lost ₦52.26 billion to fraud in 2024, showing a worrying upward trend.

‎

‎With the 2027 elections approaching, government digital systems may face even more targeted disruptions. Deloitte’s Nigeria Cybersecurity Outlook 2026 warns that ransomware and phishing will likely increase as more services and records move online.

‎RECENT NIGERIA-FOCUSED CYBER INCIDENTS


‎High-profile breaches have hit close to home recently:

‎


‎1. The Corporate Affairs Commission (CAC) faced unauthorised access, with attackers claiming to have exfiltrated millions of corporate records and over 750GB of data. This forced the temporary suspension of its company registration portal.

‎

‎2. Sterling Bank and Remita came under scrutiny after alleged coordinated attacks exposed customer data, including BVNs, NINs, passports, and other KYC documents. The Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC) launched investigations into these incidents, with reports mentioning up to 3TB of data leaked in one case.

‎

‎3. Other government agencies, fintech platforms, and financial institutions have also been targeted in waves of ransomware and data exfiltration attempts.

‎

‎These cases prove that no sector — banking, government, telecom, or small businesses — is completely safe.

‎

‎WHY INDIVIDUALS AND SMEs ARE MOST VULNERABLE

‎

‎Everyday Nigerians and small-to-medium enterprises (SMEs) often bear the brunt because they lack enterprise-level defenses. Many rely heavily on mobile devices for banking and business, reuse weak passwords, and easily fall for urgent “alerts” via WhatsApp or SMS. With limited awareness training and tight budgets, basic protections are frequently overlooked, making them easy targets for low-skilled attackers armed with AI tools.

‎COMMON CYBER THREATS TARGETING NIGERIANS IN 2026

‎

‎Here are the dominant threats:


‎1. AI-Powered Phishing & Social Engineering

‎Fake alerts from “your bank,” NITDA, or INEC via email, SMS, or WhatsApp. Deepfakes now make voice and video impersonation alarmingly realistic.

‎

‎2. Ransomware Attacks

‎These lock systems and disrupt operations for businesses, hospitals, and payment platforms, affecting salaries, taxes, and public services.

‎

‎3. Credential Stuffing & Account Takeovers

‎Password stealers thrive because many Nigerians reuse the same password across banking and social media accounts.

‎

‎4. Business Email Compromise (BEC) & SIM-Swap Scams

‎These lead to massive financial losses for individuals and companies through fake invoice payments or unauthorized transfers.

‎

‎5. Mobile and Fintech Vulnerabilities

‎Unsecured smartphones used for daily transactions become prime entry points as digital payments continue to explode.

‎10 PRACTICAL STEPS TO PROTECT YOURSELF AND YOUR BUSINESS

‎

‎Strong cybersecurity doesn’t require expensive tools — consistent habits make the biggest difference:

‎


‎1. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA/2FA) Everywhere — Prioritize banking apps, email, CAC portal, and social accounts.

‎

‎2. Use Strong, Unique Passwords — Adopt a reputable password manager instead of using names, birthdays, or “password123”.

‎

‎3. Keep All Software and Apps Updated — Turn on automatic updates for phones, laptops, apps, and routers to patch known vulnerabilities.

‎

‎4. Verify Before You Click or Share — Always double-check unexpected requests. Call the official number rather than replying to suspicious messages.

‎

‎5. Install Reputable Security Software — Use updated antivirus with real-time protection across all devices.

‎

‎6. Adopt a Zero-Trust Mindset — Verify every access request, especially for remote work or shared cloud services.

‎

‎7. Backup Your Data Regularly — Follow the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, on 2 different media types, with 1 offsite or in the cloud. Test your backups periodically.

‎

‎8. Educate Yourself and Your Team — Conduct simple awareness sessions on recognising social engineering, deepfake calls, and urgent “CEO fraud” demands.

‎

‎9. Secure Mobile Banking and Wi-Fi — Avoid public Wi-Fi for sensitive transactions. Use a VPN on public networks and avoid public USB chargers.

‎

‎10. Monitor Accounts and Report Promptly — Review bank statements and BVN activity weekly. Report incidents quickly to the Nigeria Police Force National Cybercrime Centre (NPF-NCCC), NDPC, or your bank.

‎

‎For SMEs: Perform regular vendor audits, enforce least-privilege access, and create a basic incident response plan.
